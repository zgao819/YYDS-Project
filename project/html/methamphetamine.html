Methamphetamine

Methamphetamine, commonly known as meth, is a potent and highly addictive stimulant that significantly harms both physical and mental health. Physically, it can cause severe cardiovascular issues, neurological damage, rapid and unhealthy weight loss, and dental problems often referred to as “meth mouth.” Chronic use also leads to skin sores and a prematurely aged appearance. Mentally, methamphetamine can result in intense addiction, paranoia, psychosis, mood disorders, and violent or risky behavior. The social consequences are equally devastating, often leading to strained relationships, financial and legal issues, and an inability to maintain employment or daily responsibilities. Meth’s effects make it a highly dangerous substance with a challenging path to recovery.

METHAMPHETAMINE

There is no safe amount or time for pregnant or breastfeeding women to use methamphetamine or "P".

Using methamphetamine can lead to these risks for mothers
  • preeclampsia or pregnancy related high blood pressure
  • placental abruption – or placenta coming away from the womb early
  • early pregnancy loss (before 26 weeks)
  • higher risk of having cardiovascular disease
  • poorer overall health
Risks for the baby
  • being born prematurely, less than 37 weeks' gestation
  • weighing less, being shorter with a smaller head circumference at birth
  • poorer neurological signs at birth such as
    • quality of movement
    • poorer ability to stay calm and alert
    • more difficult to arouse and attend to their environment which may impact early learning
Exposure to methamphetamine can lead to ongoing developmental problems
  • poor growth and delays in learning to walk, and learning to use their hands and fingers to manipulate objects, use a crayon or pencil, grasp the handle of a cup
  • poorer inhibitory control or “impulse control”
  • poorer working memory or the ability to remember and use information for a short period of time, such as remembering an address or a phone number
  • poorer attention
Not all effects of methamphetamine and other drug use observed in mothers and babies is due to methamphetamine use alone. Misuse of maternal methamphetamine or other drugs worldwide is associated with inadequate prenatal care, poor nutrition, poverty, mental illness, a history of sexual or physical abuse.

Children exposed to methamphetamine and other drugs during pregnancy are also likely to be exposed to a home where there are similar risks. These risks will further impact healthy development and wellbeing.

It is never too late to reduce the risks of alcohol and other drug use for you and your baby, so talk to someone…your whānau, your midwife, or your doctor. For information on where to get further help here are some links.

Help with mental health and addiction

If you're in immediate danger, call 111 and ask for the police. If it's unsafe to speak, push 55 on a mobile (or any number on a landline) to be put through to the police.
