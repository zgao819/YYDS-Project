Alcohol

If you are pregnant or planning on a pregnancy not drinking alcohol is the safest option. During pregnancy, alcohol can be transferred to your baby through the placenta and umbilical cord affecting your pēpi's development. Drinking during pregnancy can lead to long-term harm to the baby, with the more you drink, the greater the risk.

Alcohol Information

There is no safe amount or time for pregnant or breastfeeding women to drink alcohol. Binge drinking which is 4 or more drinks within 2 hours or chronic heavy drinking which is 3 or more drinks per day can cause the most harm.

Using large amounts of alcohol can lead to these risks for mothers
  • preeclampsia or pregnancy related high blood pressure
  • gestational weight gain greater than recommended for healthy mothers
  • placental abruption or placenta coming away from the womb early
  • miscarriage
  • dehydration, more frequent vomiting and overall poor health
Risks for the baby
  • being born prematurely, less than 37 weeks' gestation
  • the baby may be still born
  • weighing less at birth, or being smaller overall
Exposure to large amounts of alcohol can lead to Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder (FASD) for the baby and these risks
  • early and lifelong physical, learning and behavioural problems
  • poor growth and physical development
  • irreversible brain damage
Not all effects of alcohol and other drug use observed in mothers and babies are due to alcohol use alone. Misuse of maternal alcohol or illicit drugs worldwide is associated with inadequate prenatal care, poor nutrition, poverty, mental illness, a history of sexual or physical abuse.

Children exposed to alcohol and other drugs during pregnancy are also likely to be exposed to a home where there are similar risks. These risks will further impact healthy development and wellbeing.

It is never too late to reduce the risks of alcohol and other drug use for you and your baby, so talk to someone…your whānau, your midwife, or your doctor. For information on where to get further help here are some links.
  • https://www.cads.org.nz/
  • https://www.waitematadhb.govt.nz/hospitals-clinics/clinics-services/cads/
  • https://www.aodcollaborative.org.nz/vdb/document/169
All recreational drugs, including smoking, taken during pregnancy can pass to the baby. Depending on the kinds of drugs used, how much and how often and when they are used, they can harm the baby's brain and organs.

Help with mental health and addiction

If you're in immediate danger, call 111 and ask for the police. If it's unsafe to speak, push 55 on a mobile (or any number on a landline) to be put through to the police.

Mental health and addiction services

You do not have to deal with a mental illness or addiction on your own — there are resources, helplines, support groups, websites and counsellors available.
